## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UniGram is a university-focused social networking application built for Ghana's campus communities. It is developed in Expo/React Native with a Supabase backend, and is positioned for simultaneous launch on the Apple App Store and Google Play Store. This Strategic Plan covers the 12-month period from May 2026 to April 2027, providing the CEO and leadership team with a phased roadmap, measurable KPIs, a structured risk register, and clear board-level recommendations.

The plan is anchored in three strategic pillars and is divided into three operational phases aligned with Ghana's university academic calendar.

---

## VISION STATEMENT

> *"To become the social infrastructure of every university campus in Ghana — the platform where students connect, collaborate, and build their futures together."*

---

## THREE STRATEGIC PILLARS

### Pillar 1: Community First
UniGram's competitive advantage is not features — it is belonging. Every product decision, every marketing message, and every partnership must deepen the sense that UniGram is a space built specifically for Ghanaian university students. This means local language considerations, campus-specific features (department feeds, lecture hall check-ins, exam countdown tools), and content moderation standards calibrated for a university community.

### Pillar 2: Trust Through Compliance
In a market where data privacy awareness is growing and app store gatekeeping is strict, legal and ethical compliance is a strategic asset, not a burden. UniGram will launch with a full suite of legal documentation, transparent data practices, and clear community guidelines. This positions the brand as responsible and professional — attributes that matter to university partnerships and future investors alike.

### Pillar 3: Scalable Growth Infrastructure
The campus ambassador model is the growth engine. Rather than relying on paid advertising (cost-prohibitive at this stage), UniGram will invest in people — student leaders who are trusted on their campuses and who derive real benefit from representing the platform. This model is scalable, culturally intelligent, and self-reinforcing once it achieves critical mass.

## RISK REGISTER

### Risk 1: Low App Store Approval / Rejection
**Probability:** Medium | **Impact:** High
**Description:** Apple and Google have increasingly stringent review processes for social apps, particularly those handling user-generated content and involving minors.
**Mitigation:** Complete all compliance documentation before submission. Include a clear age gate (18+), a published privacy policy URL, detailed reviewer notes explaining the app's purpose and user verification mechanism. Allow a 3–4 week buffer before target launch date.

*Assigned: Akosua Frimpong + CEO*

---

### Risk 2: Low User Retention Post-Install
**Probability:** Medium | **Impact:** High
**Description:** Users download the app but do not return after the first session — a common failure mode for new social platforms with thin content networks.
**Mitigation:** Seed the platform with content before public launch (ambassador-posted content, department boards, events). Implement push notification onboarding sequence. Focus Phase 1 on a single university to ensure density before spreading thin.

*Assigned: CEO + Dev Lead*

---

### Risk 3: Data Breach or Privacy Incident
**Probability:** Low | **Impact:** Very High
**Description:** A data breach involving student personal information would be catastrophic for trust, legally actionable under Ghana's Data Protection Act 2023, and potentially fatal for the company.
**Mitigation:** Enforce row-level security in Supabase. Never store plaintext passwords. Implement regular security audits. Execute a Data Processing Agreement with Supabase. Prepare an Incident Response Plan before launch.

*Assigned: Akosua Frimpong + Dev Lead*

---

### Risk 4: Competitor Response from Established Platforms
**Probability:** High | **Impact:** Medium
**Description:** Meta (Instagram/WhatsApp), TikTok, and Twitter/X could improve their campus features or launch targeted campaigns in Ghana to counter UniGram's growth narrative.
**Mitigation:** UniGram's defensibility lies in its vertical focus and local identity. Compete on depth, not breadth. Build features that global platforms cannot or will not build for a Ghanaian university audience — university verification, GES academic calendar integration, SRC notice boards.

*Assigned: Ebo Hammond + CEO*

---

### Risk 5: Funding Shortfall / Runway Exhaustion
**Probability:** Medium | **Impact:** High
**Description:** Without external funding, the company's ability to invest in growth, pay for infrastructure, or attract talent is severely constrained.
**Mitigation:** Minimise burn rate during Phase 1–2 by leveraging Supabase's free tier, Expo's open-source tooling, and volunteer/ambassador labour. Begin investor outreach in Phase 2 (October 2026) so funding is secured before Phase 3 revenue ramp. Apply to TEF and Ghana Innovative Fund.

*Assigned: Nana Darkoa + CEO*

---

### Risk 6: Misuse, Harassment, and Community Safety Incidents
**Probability:** Medium | **Impact:** High
**Description:** As a social platform, UniGram is exposed to cyberbullying, harassment, misinformation, and inappropriate content — especially in a university context where students may face academic and personal pressures.
**Mitigation:** Launch with clear Community Guidelines. Build a straightforward in-app reporting mechanism. Designate a content moderation responsibility (initially the CEO or a trusted ambassador). Escalation protocol for serious incidents (e.g., mental health crises) must be documented before launch.

*Assigned: CEO + Ebo Hammond*

## BOARD RECOMMENDATIONS TO THE CEO

1. **Do not rush the launch.** A week's delay to get compliance right is worth more than a rushed listing that gets rejected or creates legal liability.

2. **KNUST first.** You are based in Kumasi. Your network, your credibility, and your proximity are strongest there. Dominate KNUST before scaling. A platform with 800 deeply engaged KNUST users is more valuable than one with 2,000 casual users spread across 10 campuses.

3. **Tell the story.** Investors, partners, and users respond to narrative. UniGram is not just an app — it is the first social platform built specifically for Ghanaian university students. That story deserves to be told confidently, consistently, and everywhere.

4. **Hire one person before you need them.** A part-time community manager or social media coordinator — possibly a KNUST communications or marketing student — will have outsized impact in Phase 2 and Phase 3. Budget for this.

5. **Measure everything from day one.** Supabase gives you the data. Use it. Weekly retention curves, daily active user trends, post engagement rates — these numbers are what convert a good pitch into a fundable pitch.
